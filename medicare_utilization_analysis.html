What is Medicare?¶

Medicare is the U.S. federal health insurance program primarily for people 65 and older, as well as some younger people with disabilities or specific conditions like end-stage kidney disease. Its main goals are:

  • Access to care: Ensure elderly and disabled Americans have access to healthcare regardless of income, since these groups often can't get affordable private insurance on their own.
  • Financial protection: Protect beneficiaries from catastrophic medical costs that could wipe out their savings because medical bills are one of the biggest causes of financial hardship for older Americans.
  • Health equity: Before Medicare was created in 1965, roughly half of seniors had no health insurance at all. Medicare dramatically reduced that gap, particularly for lower-income and minority populations.
  • Supporting the healthcare system: Because Medicare covers such a large share of the population, it also serves as a major driver of healthcare policy. Therefore, it sets reimbursement rates, quality standards, and payment models that the broader healthcare industry tends to follow.

There are four main parts to Medicare.

  • Part A: Hospital insurance (inpatient stays, skilled nursing, hospice)
  • Part B: Medical insurance (doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services)
  • Part C: Medicare Advantage (private insurance plans that bundle A + B, sometimes with extras like dental/vision)
  • Part D: Prescription drug coverage

These can be split into two main sections:

  • FFS (Fee-for-Service): Parts A and B together, traditional Medicare, where the government pays per service
  • MA (Medicare Advantage): Part C, a private plan alternative

These sections' definitions will be significant in future parts of this report.

Explanation of Data¶

The data used in this project is taken directly from the CMS.gov website. As listed on the website, CMS is "the federal agency that provides health coverage to more than 160 million through Medicare, Medicaid, the Children's Health Insurance Program, and the Health Insurance Marketplace. CMS works in partnership with the entire health care community to improve quality, equity and outcomes in the health care system." Our data is specifically taken from MCBS (Medicare Current Beneficiary Survey), which is a survey of actual Medicare beneficiaries that collects information on their health, demographics, and how much healthcare they use. It is taken each year periodically during each season of the year. This data is from 2020-2023 and uses the surveys taken in the fall and winter periods of each year. More information can be found about the data and where it is from here: https://www.cms.gov/data-research/research/medicare-current-beneficiary-survey

There is also a codebook attached to this assignment that explains each variable in greater detail. These codebooks were taken directly from the website.

PCA-Based Utilization Score¶

Now that the variables have been seperated into utilization varaibles and predictor variables, I can create a utilization score for each datapoint through a process called PCA. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a dimensionality reduction technique. When you have many correlated variables (like multiple utilization measures), PCA finds new summary variables that are uncorrelated with each other and capture as much of the original variation as possible.

# Create PCA utilization score
pca_scores = pca_full.transform(util_scaled)
pc1_raw = pca_scores[:, 0]

# Flip sign if needed so higher = more utilization
raw_total = util_df[count_cols].sum(axis=1) + util_df[binary_int_cols + binary_obj_cols].sum(axis=1)
if np.corrcoef(pc1_raw, raw_total)[0, 1] < 0:
    pc1_raw = -pc1_raw

# Normalize 0-100, then log-transform
pc1_norm = ((pc1_raw - pc1_raw.min()) / (pc1_raw.max() - pc1_raw.min()) * 100).round(2)

These models also used cross-validation, which is shown in the CV R^2 mean and CV R^2 std parts of the table. The type of cross-validation I used was a k-fold one with n=5 folds. This split the data into 5 different sections of data and ran the model on all of these. The CV R^2 mean tells us what the mean of each fold was. Since the means for our best performing models were close to the test R^2, we know that the CV worked and the models did not have an unrepresentative subsample. The same goes for CV R^2 std, which has a very low std for all models, meaning there wasn't much variance between the different folds. This gives us the same conclusion as the CV R^2 mean.

In terms of the validity of the model, the overfit gap for both boosting models is relatively low, showing that the stratification worked and it is not a fluke. The CV R^2 is also low for all models, meaning the k-fold cross-validation worked too.

def predict_with_explanation(patient_data: dict, n_reasons: int = 5):
    input_df = pd.DataFrame([patient_data])[high_cols]
    log_pred = best_tree_model.predict(input_df)[0]
    raw_pred = np.expm1(log_pred)
    sv = explainer.shap_values(input_df)[0]
    reasons = sorted(zip(high_cols, sv, input_df.values[0]),
                     key=lambda x: abs(x[1]), reverse=True)[:n_reasons]
    return {
        "predicted_utilization_score": round(raw_pred, 2),
        "risk_level": "HIGH" if raw_pred > 50 else "MODERATE" if raw_pred > 20 else "LOW",
        "top_factors": [{"feature": f, "impact": round(float(s), 4),
            "direction": "↑ increases" if s > 0 else "↓ decreases",
            "patient_value": float(v)} for f, s, v in reasons]
    }

Conclusion¶

During the process of this project, I have transformed thousands and thousands of lines of data into actionable insights simply by running it through a machine learning model. Through preprocessing, testing, and computing, I have determined some important information using the XGBoost Regressor model to determine the causes of using Medicare.

Now, up until this point, most of the narration of this project has been fairly technical and has only explained what each section of the code is doing without putting it all together. Now, in this section of the report, I will put together all of my findings in a meaningful way that encompasses all sections and provides an overall understanding of the project.

I found that the Gradient Boosting Model and XGBoosting Model had the best results for the data with the highest R^2s. The worst performing was the Lasso and ElasticNet with the lowest R^2s. This was not surprising since the two boosting models are fairly advanced and are normally the highest performers. Lasso and ElasticNet kinda sucks so it makes sense.

The conclusion of the data showed one variable consistently stood out as the best predictor of utilization score. This variable was ADM_FFS_FLAG_YR. It's a flag variable for whether the beneficiary used Fee-for-Service or Medicare Advantage for that year. Fee-for-Service encompasses parts A and B of Medicare that are defined at the beginning of this project. These parts are hospital insurance and medicare insurance. The government pays directly for each individual doctor visit or hospital visit that the beneficiary goes to. This is different from Medicare Advantage, which is a blanket insurance where the government pays the insurer a fixed amount for medical services.

This distinction is important because we can tell the ADM_FFS_FLAG_YR variable tells us whether or not the person used Fee-for-Service or Medicare Advantage. Since this variable has so much influence on predicting the target variable across all methods, we can deduce that the same health conditions predict high utilization differently depending on whether a beneficiary is in Fee-for-Service or Medicare Advantage, which means one-size-fits-all care management doesn't work. The plan cannot just loop everyone into the same plan because it will be detrimental to how effectively Medicare is utilized.

Additionally, ACW_CARESPCL is the variable that tells us if the person uses a specialist outside the primary health provider. Since it is the second-highest predictor in the model, we can understand that people who use outside specialists also utilize Medicare more than those who don't. Knowing this, we can infer that it would be a good idea for Medicare policies to continue funding and supporting medical specialists so that Medicare can continue being effective and not allow it to go to waste.

Lastly, DEM_AGE is the other high predictor that all 3 of the methods used to predict utilization. From this, we can understand that a person's age plays a big role in determining if they utilize Medicare or not. This makes sense since older people generally have more health problems than younger people. This shows us that the model is working! It is getting a basic, intuitive part of the model correct and helps us confirm that these findings are not just random.

Overall, my biggest takeaways from this project were a better understanding of Medicare and how it worked. I originally didn't know how the system worked and what it did specifically, but after combing through variables, choosing predictors, and constructing a utilization score, I have a better idea of what it does. This project has been meaningful to me and has also helped me understand the medical problems that people face later in life. It helps me to be more compassionate and understanding of people's situations that are fairly far removed from my college bubble.
